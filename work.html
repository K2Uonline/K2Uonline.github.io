Since graduating from UCSD in 2020, I've worked at two large government contracting defense engineering companies, Leidos and L3Harris. I've really enjoyed my time at bothe of them; they've given me lots of oppurtunities and taught me how to be an effective engineer. At Leidos, I was grateful for having coworkers who were expert electrical engineers with 40+ years of experince and who treated me with morer espect than I deserved. They never talked down to me nor brushed me aside. They held me to high expectations for a first year engineer and were willing to answer every question I asked of them. I worked on designing control systems for large vehicle X-ray portals. My proudest design was a refreshing the main timing microcontroller packet sender system. The board that I spun connected the X-ray source I/O, X-ray detector data, all the interlocks in the system, and the user PC. I focused on making the control system modular in order to future-proof it by having the microcontroller (STM32) and power live on the main board with 2 80 pin Samtec headers going to an I/O board that was specific to the X-ray source (LINAC) and detectors. This allowed us to use the same microncontroller power system for our other systems that used different X-ray sources (we used a lot of betatrons in other systems) in the future. It was very rewarding to see my design implemented in an actual sold and installed security product. A smaller project I got to work on was a communications electrical to optical transceiver. It could convert between single ended TTL, differential RS-232, and optical logic to send the clocking packets between the X-ray source and detectors. Having this experience with optical signals led to my next position at L3Harris working on optics based microelectronics. At L3Harris, I was excited to work on cutting edge technology.
